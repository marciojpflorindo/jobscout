# Profile (example)

> This is a generic placeholder. Your real `profile.md` is generated by the
> first-run interview and is gitignored — it never gets committed. You can
> hand-edit `profile.md` any time, or re-run onboarding to rebuild it.
>
> The structure below is what the brain reads to judge each job posting.

## Who this is for

A short description of you, in your own words. Example:

> A mid-level backend developer with 5 years in Python and Go, looking to move
> into a remote-first team building developer tooling.

## Target paths (ranked)

What you want, best first. Example:

1. Senior Backend Engineer (Python / Go) — remote
2. Platform / Infrastructure Engineer — remote or hybrid
3. Developer Experience / Tooling Engineer

## Search terms

The queries the brain feeds to the job boards. Example:

- backend engineer
- platform engineer
- python developer

## Location & remote preference

- **Country:** (e.g. Germany)
- **City (optional):** (e.g. Berlin)
- **Remote preference:** remote-only | hybrid-ok | on-site

## Tier-A — hard blockers (instant no)

Deal-breakers that disqualify a job outright. Example:

- Requires relocation outside my country
- Requires a security clearance I don't have
- Companies to exclude: (e.g. CompanyX, CompanyY)
- Industries to avoid: (e.g. gambling, adtech)

## Tier-B — judge by duties

Softer signals to weigh from the posting's actual responsibilities. Example:

- Favour roles with real engineering depth over coordination-only roles
- Prefer product teams over agency/consultancy churn
- Seniority should match: mid-to-senior, not lead/manager-only

## Scoring rubric (0–100)

How the model should turn its judgement into a score. Example:

- **80–100** — strong match on target path + duties, no blockers
- **50–79** — plausible, some gaps or mismatch worth a look
- **1–49** — weak match; surfaced low or not at all
- **disqualified** — hits a Tier-A blocker, regardless of other fit
